Accelerating Photosensitized Drug Design with Graph Neural Networks

09.2025

During the summer, I worked on a project focused on accelerating sample preparation for photosensitized drug design assays using graph neural networks (GNNs). The goal was to replace slow, resource-intensive prediction pipelines with a scalable, data-driven alternative.

Photosensitized reactions play an important role in drug discovery, medical imaging, and the study of cell membranes. Gaining a better understanding of these processes can directly support the development of new therapeutics and diagnostic tools.

Traditionally, predicting photosensitization properties requires extensive manual analysis by domain experts and can take days per compound. The model I developed enables thousands of predictions per second while achieving approximately 80% accuracy—performance that previously required substantial human effort. This represents a significant improvement in speed and scalability for early-stage screening.

The core idea is to represent molecules as graphs, where atoms correspond to nodes and chemical bonds to edges. The neural network learns directly from this structure, incorporating a wide range of molecular descriptors to approximate real-world chemical behavior. In effect, the approach creates a lightweight “digital laboratory” in which molecular sensitization can be explored computationally.

As part of this work, I also explored UMA, Meta’s large-scale molecular simulator trained on over 100 million molecules, which provided valuable insights and complementary perspectives on molecular representation and simulation.

This project was carried out in collaboration with NexMR. I am grateful to Felix Torres Hubiche and the team for their guidance and for the opportunity to contribute to a project with real medical relevance.

Ongoing and future work. My next project will focus on accelerating the computation of quantum descriptors. The resulting tools will be released as open-source software.
